Define a metric : Box office revenue

"In order to run a meaningful correlation analysis of violence in films on real-world violence, you need to think of your independent variable as a treatment", said Ada. For this, our classification of films in violent and peaceful comes in handy! However, this was only the first step. We need more information about the movies that we classified as violent! So how can we follow Ada's advice?

Let's break it down this way: Simply counting the number of violent movies released in a specific timespan does not seem to fully grasp the complexity of our question of interest. A good intention for this statement is the following: If there are a lot of violent movies released, but nobody watches them, we do not assume those movies to have a large influence on the society. On the other hand, if there is only one violent movie released, but nearly everybody watches it, it could have a significant influence on society! Having this in mind, we search for a variable that could capture the popularity of the violent movies released.

For this, one information seems quite suitable: the box office revenue! The box office revenue is highly correlated with the popularity of a movie, i.e. it is high when a lot of people watched the movie (popular movie) and low when only a few people watched it (unpopular movie). Using the box office revenue of the films we classified as violent, we can thus approximate the prevalence of violent movies during a specific time period. Therefore, we choose the box office revenue as the treatment, i.e. our main independent variable, for the correlation analysis!

We now need to create a metric for the violent score. Counts, ratio, z-scores normalisation, min-max normalisation,... The list could go on! But since we are interested in the time effect, we have to be careful not to include information from the future. Indeed, z-score taking into account all the available data as we did before is great to analyse the trends but, as soon as we move in time domain analysis, it results in errors.
"Indeed, it would be like doing time-travels, but we are not Marty and Dr. Emmet", points out Yodada, referencing Back to the future.

via GIPHY

"Let’s do a first approximate by summing all counts per week, no matter the category. I will dwelve in the subtle details of time-related data later."
This approach allows a general picture but have the huge drawback to consider all offenses category equally violent and do not take care of any imbalanced.
"Let's take an example. Let imagine in week X 36 assaults have been commited but no homicide. Then week X+1, 1 assault and 35 murders has been commited. Those two weeks would appear equally violent, when their pattern isn't at all the same! How to correct this ? Z-score normalisation per category !"
"But you mentionned that we couldn't look into the future to process our data...", said Gator.
"Well done! You're a quick learner. Indeed we can't simply take the mean and stantard deviation of the whole period consider. But this can be solved with the concept of past causal window. This means that for a given position t in the time, the rolling window includes only the current value and the values prior to it. It therefore preserves the natural timescale of the events while scaling them so that they are comparable."

We again observe again patterns corresponding to weeks where a particular event arise. This confirms that we haven’t distorted our real-life violence data! Note that, to enable their use in our model, we will aggregate them into a single value per week for each year consider.
